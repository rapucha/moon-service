Moon path design follow-up

Sun marker mockups

These variants make the Sun marker materially larger than the first simple marker and closer to the Alternative Moon marker scale. Each option keeps the Sun visually secondary to the Moon path while giving photographers clearer altitude and azimuth context.

Scale target

Move from tiny glyph to chart object

The current simple Sun marker is about half the visual mass of the Alternative Moon marker. These mockups target a 25 to 29 px core-plus-corona footprint, with halos near the Alternative Moon halo size.

A. Corona disk Preferred

Closest to the Alternative Moon scale without looking like another Moon. The larger warm disk, eight short rays, and inner highlight stay legible at chart size.

B. Horizon glow Low-sun feel

Uses a larger disk with soft triangular rays and two subtle horizon arcs. It reads more photographic, especially for sunrise and golden-hour samples.

C. Compass notch Azimuth cue

Adds a small northeast-facing notch to make the marker feel directional. It is useful for azimuth context, but the extra point may compete with dense chart labels.

D. Aperture explorations Photographer cue

Reworked aperture-style options with wider openings and less internal clutter. The chart compares open grooves, soft blades, long rays, and a dark-core aperture at real sample size.

  • D1 Wide groovesLargest bright opening without muddy filled blades.
  • D2 Wide bladesKeeps blade geometry, but leaves a broader clear center.
  • D3 Wide raysMost Sun-like at small size, now with a larger aperture center.
  • D4 Dark apertureBright disk with a darker opening to read more like a camera aperture.

Implementation notes

  • All proposed Sun markers use a footprint close to the Alternative Moon marker, but still smaller than the Best Moon marker.
  • The chart should continue to omit negative-altitude Sun positions and Sun positions above the maximum displayed Moon altitude.
  • Variant A is the cleanest production candidate; variant B is the most photographic; variant C emphasizes azimuth; variant D now compares wider aperture-inspired treatments, including a darker opening inside a bright Sun disk.
